Gothic Architecture

Originating in 12th-century France, Gothic architecture is characterized by **pointed arches**, **ribbed vaults**, and **flying buttresses**. These elements allowed builders to achieve unprecedented height and fill interiors with light, famously seen in great cathedrals like Notre-Dame de Paris. It represents a major leap in structural engineering and aesthetic expression of the medieval period.

The Bauhaus Movement

The Bauhaus school, founded in 1919 by Walter Gropius, fundamentally shifted modern design. Its philosophy was "form follows function," advocating for the unity of art and industrial design. Bauhaus architecture features **flat roofs**, **smooth facades**, and **cubic shapes**, embodying rational, functional aesthetics ideal for the machine age. Its influence is global and enduring.
